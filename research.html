Work in Progress

Pricing and Transmission of Climate Risk in Corporate Loan (with Max Yang)
Abstract

Local natural disasters are geographically concentrated shocks, yet credit markets are nationally and globally integrated. This paper studies whether physical climate risk remains local or propagates through financial intermediaries to affect credit markets more broadly. Using syndicated loan data and variation in borrowers’ ex ante hurricane exposure, we compare borrowers in high- and low-exposure counties following major U.S. hurricanes while excluding counties directly affected by the disaster. We find that loan spreads increase significantly for more exposed borrowers after hurricanes, even in the absence of direct damage. At the same time, loan size and syndicate participation decline, indicating a tightening of credit supply. The repricing of risk is concentrated among financial firms, while non-financial firms adjust primarily along non-price margins. Overall, the results show that localized climate disasters generate economy-wide financial spillovers through credit markets.

Physical Climate Risk and Heterogeneous Investment Responses
Abstract

This paper examines how firm-level exposure to physical climate risk alters the investment response to monetary policy. I construct a novel panel dataset combining quarterly Compustat financial statements with annual climate sensitivity scores from Trucost for more than 2,000 U.S. firms between 2019 and 2023. Climate sensitivity indicators—standardized across multiple hazards such as floods, wildfires, and heatwaves—are interacted with monetary policy shocks to estimate heterogeneous effects. I find that firms facing greater physical climate sensitivity invest significantly less in response to expansionary monetary policy. This attenuation persists across alternative fixed-effects specifications and across different hazard types, suggesting that climate sensitivity acts as a friction in the transmission of monetary policy. These results highlight how rising physical climate risk can weaken the effectiveness of monetary policy and have important implications for macro-financial stability in a changing climate.

Pre-Doctoral Publications

Ex-post Evaluation of the R&D Tax Incentive Law in Chile (with Cristian Mardones)
Abstract

This study carries out an ex - post evaluation of the Research and Development (R&D) Tax Incentive Law in Chile. A subset of data from the 9th Innovation Survey is chosen, specifically those that were available for the years 2013 and 2014. Then, differences in differences (DID) and matching with differences in differences (MDID) techniques are used to identify the impact of this policy. The results obtained allow us to affirm that the use of the R&D Tax Incentive Law had some positive but very low effects on some components of expenditure for innovation. Also, the positive effect of the tax credits on the total expenditure for innovation identified with MDID disappears when only firms that know the R&D Tax Incentive Law are used as a control group. This work provides new evidence to evaluate innovation policies in Latin America, focusing on tax credits that have been much less studied than subsidy programs. Specifically, it is concluded that there is still a wide margin to improve and reformulate the R&D Tax Incentive Law in Chile.
