Selected Work in Progress

Pricing and Transmission of Climate Risk in Corporate Lending (with Max Yang)

Local natural disasters are geographically concentrated shocks, yet credit markets are nationally and globally integrated, raising the question of whether physical climate risk remains local or propagates through the financial system. This paper studies how major U.S. hurricanes affect credit pricing and allocation by exploiting variation in borrowers’ ex ante hurricane exposure using syndicated loan data and a difference-in-differences design that excludes directly damaged counties. We find that loan spreads increase significantly for more exposed borrowers following hurricanes, accompanied by declines in loan size and syndicate participation, indicating a tightening of credit conditions driven by changes in perceived risk rather than realized losses. A key contribution is to document pronounced heterogeneity across borrower types: the repricing of credit risk is concentrated among financial firms, which face large and immediate increases in borrowing costs, while non-financial firms exhibit little spread adjustment and instead adjust primarily along non-price margins. From the lender perspective, we show that banks with greater exposure to hurricane risk reallocate credit away from risky areas toward safer locations through funding- and asset-side balance-sheet channels, while nonbank lenders adjust primarily along non-price margins and partially absorb credit demand in exposed regions. Overall, the results demonstrate that localized climate disasters generate economy-wide financial spillovers through credit markets, with important implications for financial stability and the regulation of climate-related risk.

Heterogeneous Firms and Physical Climate Risk

This paper studies whether exposure to physical climate risk weakens the investment channel of monetary policy. I construct a novel firm–quarter panel by merging Compustat North America financial statements with Trucost (S&P Global) firm-level physical climate risk scores for 2,487 publicly listed U.S. firms over 2015–2023. I exploit exogenous variation in the user cost of capital using high-frequency monetary policy shocks and interact these shocks with firm-level physical risk to identify heterogeneous investment responses in a saturated fixed-effects specification. I find that firms with higher physical risk exposure are significantly less responsive in investment following expansionary monetary policy shocks: a one-standard-deviation increase in physical risk reduces the semi-elasticity of investment with respect to a one–percentage-point policy rate cut by about 4 percent, and the response is concentrated among low-risk firms. The results suggest that rising physical climate risk can generate unequal investment across firms and attenuate the effectiveness of standard countercyclical monetary policy in high-risk regions and sectors.
